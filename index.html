On top of detention and apprehension, the legal system poses major challenges.
There is a backlog of 1.8 million immigration court cases. 0.6 million cases were added in this past year.
On average, each of these court cases will take two and a half years to come to a decision.[]
Only 20.9% of immigrants, including unaccompanied children, had an attorney to assist them when a removal was ordered.[]
Of those granted relief in their case, only 6% were not represented. Access to legal services matters. []
The court is required to provide a list of immigration lawyers, but often that list has lawyers who only work with asylum seekers or those who have not been detained.
In July 2019, the Supreme Court has also expanded deportation by approving of a fast tracked way of processing undocumented people, in order to make things more "efficient".

When we think of undocumented populations, who comes to mind? The narrative fed through media, politicians, etc. can be misrepresentative of the actual numbers.

Most people associate the Latinx community with undocumented status. While 67% of the undocumented population was born in Mexico and Central America, millions of others are from other regions. Undocumented people born in Asia is the next largest subgroup, ranking at 15% of the undocumented population-- a little over 1.5 million people.[]

Perhaps this data of the racial breakdown of undocumented immigrants comes from a place rooted in biased actions. In 2021, the number of apprehensions at the US-Mexico border reached an all time high of over 1.6 million. This accounted for 97% of the total apprehensions made at borders. There must be overrepresentation of the data at the US-Mexico border.

Beyond race, gender can also be a source of harm while facing the system of immigration, especially in detention. Trangender undocumented immigrants have often fled unsafe conditions in their home country, only to be retraumatized in the American detention center. Roxana Hernandez (33) fled Honduras to escape gender based violence only to be met with medical negligence and torture at an ICE detention facility, resulting in her death. Up to 50,000 transgender undocumented immigrants live in the US. How many more must die? []

In the midst of all the terror and harm that's being inflicted upon you, you resort to leaving the place you've known as home with hope for a better life. []

How do you begin to tackle a system wired against you? Without resources like money, time, information, contacts, etc.? What violence would you face? What grief and anxiety would your family endure, living their lives in constant fear? What sort of life would you hope to make, if you were ever granted safety?

What if a better world were possible?

A better world is possible, and it's closer at hand than you might expect. It can be achieved in our lifetime. Let's talk about what that world would look like and how we can make it.

To solve issues like this, the US is quick to throw on bandaid policies. ICE's Alternatives to Detention (ATD) is one such program. Though ATD is not a 'substitute for detention' but is used to 'supervise' and 'manage compliance,' it is another means of surveillance in predominately communities of color.

Almost 300,000 individuals and families are in the program. The government collects location data and biometric data through facial recognition and listening to calls to develop voice recognition. Participants will be randomly checked on throughout the day to send a photo or pick up a call-- a form of harassment and one that costs immigrants on average $7.29 per day.

Government spending on ATD increased by more than double between 2020 and 2021. Many of those dollars go straight to tech companies who build the tools for ICE. Technologists have the responsibility to recognize their role in building these products.

As you may have noticed in the previous sections, migration is an ever growing issue and our current legal and legal-technical systems are not equipped to address these issues.

How do we address it then? Simple, taking from one of my favorite quotes by Professor Ruha Benjamin, "Remember to imagine and craft the worlds you cannot live without, just as you dismantle the worlds you cannot live within." So, how do we reimagine immigration?

The heart of our problem is that America tries to solve it by working within the current legal and socioeconomic system, no matter how nonsensical or discriminatory that approach may be for the situation. This only expands modes of discrimination and entrenches us in the problem.
We spend over billions per year apprehending, detaining, maintaining, prosecuting, harming millions of people, believing it is making America safer. It's an illusion and a means to violate the safety of others who have sacrificed, trusted and believed in this country's ideals. [] Meanwhile, the Violence Against Women Act sets aside about half a billion for prevention. Why? Why is there infinite money for idiotic catch-and-release policies that guarantee re-victimization, when we all know that the real solution is a path to safety for victims of domestic violence?

If America calls itself the Free World, why are our borders-- invisible lines-- harming and gatekeeping others? Our conceptualization of borders is what keeps us operating within a system rigged against immigrants. What would happen if we reimagined what borders mean? What the movement of people throughout the globe looked like?

A better world is possible.
